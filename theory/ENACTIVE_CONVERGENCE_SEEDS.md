# Enactive Convergence: Four Seeds on Horizon, Confabulation, and the Mall

**Date:** 2026-03-12  
**Status:** Seed — let it sit  
**Context:** Response to Zhang & Zahidi (2026), "The radical enactive account of memory errors," *Synthese* 207(2):63. DOI: 10.1007/s11229-026-05439-7. And Jin (2026), "Embedding mental files in the world," *Mind and Language* 42(2):1–21.

## I. Learned Helplessness as a Horizon Model Failure

> *"Starving to death in an unlocked room because you checked the door and you thought it was locked when it was just stuck after a lifetime of locked doors."*

The standard MBD Horizon analysis holds that irreversible memory failure occurs when B(t) has drifted so far from its encoding context that the original state is topologically unreachable within H. That is a failure of the Horizon itself — the state no longer exists within the reachable region.

This is a different and more subtle failure: **the agent's model of H is incorrect, but H is intact.** The door is unlocked. The state "escape" exists in H. The agent does not attempt re-engagement because the accumulated baseline encodes a strong prior that the door is locked. The prediction is settled before it is tested. λ-updating on the locked-door experience has been so persistent and consistent that the novelty signal for "try the door" approaches zero — the agent has already computed the outcome. The very act of checking feels redundant, not exploratory.

This is learned helplessness as a Zipfian collapse in the action space. The agent's effective H has not shrunk — the full H remains available — but the sampling probability over H has been so severely weighted by prior B(t) that regions known to have returned null are never re-sampled. It is not that the exit doesn't exist in H. It is that the agent's B(t) has effectively assigned it probability zero, and there is no internal mechanism to override that assignment without an external perturbation.

The radical enactive account cannot distinguish this case from genuine Horizon contraction. Both look the same behaviorally: the agent does not attempt the action. MBD makes a testable distinction: in learned helplessness, external scaffolding (someone else tries the door; the agent observes a different agent succeed) can restore the path, because the state exists in H. In true Horizon contraction (cachexia analog), external scaffolding does not restore access — the state is gone regardless of demonstration.

The therapeutic prediction is different. You treat learned helplessness by perturbing the prior — by creating a sufficiently novel I(t) that forces a B(t+1) update. You treat Horizon contraction differently, if at all. Confusing the two is a clinical failure mode.

## II. The Epistemic Boundary: You Cannot Have a Theory at the Edge

> *"You shouldn't have a theory on the edge of the horizon's form because it's hallucinatory, as improbable as the ground I stand on day to day."*

H is defined as the set of states representable by the agent. The edge of H — the boundary between what can and cannot be represented — is by definition the region where representation is failing. Any theoretical claim about the precise form of the boundary is made from *inside* H, using representational resources that are themselves bounded by H. The boundary cannot be observed directly from the inside. It can only be inferred from the shape of the failures at the periphery.

This is an epistemic constraint on the framework, not just on the agents it models.

The ground you stand on is improbable in the same sense: the atoms are mostly empty space, the stability is a statistical artifact, the solidity is a low-resolution encoding of something far stranger. The ground *works* — you can stand on it — but any theory of "what the ground actually is at the smallest scale" is constructed from representations that are already imprecise at that scale. The framework can model behavior near the Horizon's edge. It should not claim to characterize the edge's structure from first principles.

Practically: the Horizon in MBD papers should be specified functionally (what it does to I(t), how it shapes encoding probability Φ(I, B, H)) rather than structurally (what it *is* as a formal object). The moment the theory over-specifies the boundary form, it is making claims that exceed the representational resources of the agents it models — and of the theorist constructing the model. That is a hallucinatory posture: the appearance of precision at a scale where precision is not available.

Leave the edge vague by design. The constraint is that the Horizon is real, that it varies between agents, and that it can change. Its exact topology is not theoretically necessary and may be actively misleading.

## III. Novelty × Coupling as the Mechanism of "Aha!" in Confabulation

> *"Novelty and coupling coefficient. It makes an 'Aha!' that makes sense to the patient internally. Scapegoat construction, lies are easier to confess than truth when it comes to painful memories."*

The standard account of the felt sense of recognition — the "Aha!" of insight — in MBD is a high-novelty event: |I(t) − B(t)| is large, the prediction error is real, and the encoding is deep. High novelty = genuine epistemic update.

But there is a pathological case. Under high κ (strong coupling to an authority — therapist, interrogator, parent, priest), the arrival of a suggested I(t) generates two simultaneous effects:

1. **κ-amplified novelty**: The suggestion acquires additional signal weight proportional to coupling strength. It *feels* like a high-novelty event even if the actual deviation |I(t) − B(t)| would be modest under zero coupling.

2. **Prediction error suppression**: The high-κ relationship authorizes the suggestion. Prediction error that would ordinarily flag the incoming I(t) as inconsistent with B(t) is damped by the coupling term. The agent does not experience the dissonance that would otherwise reject the suggestion.

The result: a false encoding that arrives with all the phenomenological characteristics of genuine insight. The "Aha!" is real as an experience. It is not real as an epistemic event. The agent cannot distinguish the two from the inside because the distinguishing mechanism — the prediction error — is the thing that has been suppressed.

Scapegoat construction follows this pattern. A painful memory that is accurately encoded in B(t) has zero novelty — it is already part of the baseline — and retrieval feels flat, aversive, familiar. It does not generate an "Aha!" A constructed confabulation that attributes cause to an external agent (the scapegoat) may have high κ-amplified novelty if arrived at under therapeutic or social pressure, and it resolves the authority coupling. The confabulation feels more true than the truth. Not because it is a better fit to B(t), but because it is a better fit to the κ-weighted authority structure.

Lies are easier to confess than truth under high-κ coercion because the confabulated lie generates a larger coupling-amplified novelty signal than the suppressed truth. The felt sense of relief upon "confession" is real. It does not track accuracy. The framework predicts this without extension.

This is the mechanism of false confession in interrogation settings, false recovered memory in certain therapeutic contexts, and scapegoat consolidation in high-κ group environments.

## IV. The Mall Paradigm's Linguistic Precondition Cannot Be Patched by Translation

> *"You actually need a satisfactory liminal space parallel that is culturally attenuated. Likely regional matchups exist in Zipfian word probability regions between languages of similar structure — but mall is not a mall is a mall is a mercado. The words, the specific words, in their language are necessary."*

The lost-in-the-mall paradigm (Loftus et al.) requires that the subject have a pre-existing schema for the target environment. The implanted I(t) can only consolidate if Φ(I, B, H) is non-zero — the encoding requires a foothold in B(t). For populations without a mall schema, that foothold does not exist.

The naive patch is to translate the paradigm: find the culturally equivalent liminal public space and substitute it. But this fails for a reason that is deeper than cultural equivalence.

The schema is not just the space — it is the **word** for the space as it exists in the specific language's Zipfian probability structure. The word "mall" in American English carries a specific probability distribution over associated concepts: fluorescent lighting, food courts, teenagers, disorientation, anchor stores, disappearing exits, the peculiar anxiety of not knowing which end you came in. Those associations are encoded in B(t) not abstractly but through the word's frequency distribution and the contexts in which it appeared in the agent's linguistic history.

"Mercado" carries a different cluster. Not just a different referent, but a different Zipfian neighborhood — different collocations, different narrative contexts, different emotional valuation. The schema isn't in the place, it's in the word's position in the language's probability space. If the languages differ structurally (Germanic compounds vs. agglutinative vs. logographic), the mapping is not even lexical — the conceptual granularity of the segmentation differs. There is no translation that preserves the schema's position in B(t), because B(t) is organized by the agent's specific linguistic history, not by a language-independent conceptual space.

The paradigm cannot be made cross-culturally valid by substitution. A paradigm that requires a specific Horizon-density precondition is not a paradigm for the general case of false memory implantation. It is a paradigm for false memory implantation in agents with sufficient Horizon density in the target domain. The literature that treats it as universal is overgeneralizing.

The MBD prediction: susceptibility to false implantation will correlate not with general suggestibility (κ alone) but with *Horizon density near the target schema*, which is a function of the subject's specific linguistic and cultural history. This is testable in principle by measuring associative centrality of the target word/concept within the subject's language prior to implantation attempts.

This is not a claim about linguistic relativity in the Sapir-Whorf sense. It is narrower: the paradigm's engineering requires a specific prior that is language-and-culture-specific, and no formal translation procedure exists to identify the correct equivalent across languages with different internal probability structures.

## V. Addendum: Jin's Deeper Claim — L-Spaces Make Sense to Beings Raised in L-Spaces

> *"He's talking about how L-spaces make logical sense to beings raised in L-spaces."*

My initial reading of Jin was too narrow. I characterized him as reinventing λ from a philosophy of language angle — natural selection tuned plasticity coefficients to match environmental statistics. But the claim is structurally deeper.

Jin is not just saying that the *content* of mental files is shaped by the local environment. He is saying that the *organizing logic* of the files — what counts as a natural kind, what property clusters cohere, what distinctions warrant their own file rather than being subsumed under another — is attuned to the organizational logic of the specific environment the agent developed in. The files make logical sense because the environment they were built in has that logic. Agents raised in a different L-space would have files with a different internal logic — not just different content, but different *structure of coherence*.

This is a direct hit on H. In MBD, H is the set of states representable by the agent. Jin is saying H is not species-universal — it is L-space-specific. The topology of H (which distinctions are crisp, which are fuzzy, what the natural joints are) was shaped by developmental embedding in a particular environment. Two agents with identical species membership but different developmental L-spaces will have Horizons with different internal geometries, not just different regions populated.

This is the correct framing for Point IV (the mall seed above). It is not just that the subject lacks "mall" as a content item in B(t). It is that the subject's H may not have the *type* of schema that "mall" instantiates — a navigable indoor commercial grid with disorientation potential — as a coherent category at all. If that schema-type doesn't exist in H, no amount of content description creates it. You cannot implant an instance of a category that H does not contain.

The difference between "lacks content" and "lacks the logical structure for that content" is the difference between an empty folder and a filesystem that doesn't support that directory type.

This also reframes the Zipfian linguistic point more precisely. The reason word substitution fails across languages is not merely that different words appear with different frequencies. It is that the categorical geometry of the file system was built by language acquisition in a specific L-space, and the logical partitions those languages carve are not isomorphic. You cannot translate "mall" to "mercado" and preserve the implantation paradigm because the category of navigable-indoor-commercial-disorientation-space may not be a native partition in the target H at all.

Jin probably does not formalize this. His frame is rational analysis + evolutionary teleology; the H-topology consequence is implicit. But it is the most powerful version of his claim, and it is directly tractable in MBD once you accept that H is developmental and L-space-specific rather than universal.

## VI. Carried Topology: A Gradient of H-Mismatch

> *"A Jomon in the Edo period would be very confused. A Native American in modern America would be very sad. Cahokians would be filled with wonder the likes of which you can't imagine, and recognize parts of themselves in what they've found. Illiterate, but not suffering from existential crisis. A Floridian in St. Louis wonders at a society based on an actual economy instead of tourism, but misses his natural springs. We're made for where we are, with a little wiggle room, before we deform in a plastic manner. We carry our environment with us, within us, expressing it on our reality, and when they differ from existing L-space topologies, you get friction. Sometimes crime."*

This is the empirical gradient that Section V predicts but does not ground. What does H-topology mismatch actually *feel like*, at different magnitudes?

**Jomon in Edo period: confusion.** Jomon H-topology is tuned to a hunting-gathering L-space — small bands, seasonal movement, animist object-relations, no rigid hierarchy, no literacy, no monetary abstraction. Edo Japan is an urban feudal system with rigid class stratification, formal written law, commodity currency, and a bureaucratic social ontology. The Jomon agent's H does not contain the *category-types* that structure Edo daily life. "Samurai" is not a content-gap — it is a missing category type. The organizational logic is alien. The result is not sadness but disorientation: the environment is full of events that do not parse.

**Native American in modern America: grief.** The H-topology here has higher overlap with modernity — post-contact transmission, shared geographic space, forced assimilation has written modern categorical structures into the Horizon. There is enough overlap to parse what is happening. The grief is not confusion but *recognition of absence* — the L-space the topology was tuned for still exists partially in language, memory, and landscape, but has been displaced from dominance. The agent can read the modern L-space but it does not feel like home. The topology fits but the territory it was built for is diminished. That is a specific kind of suffering with no confabulation available to resolve it.

**Cahokians in modern America: wonder with partial recognition.** Cahokia had urban density, long-range trade networks, monumental architecture, a cosmological-administrative system, social stratification, and a civic identity. The organizational logic is not alien to modernity — they built cities, they managed populations, they coordinated labor at scale. The H-topology has enough structural overlap to generate recognition: *this is a kind of thing I have a category for, just enormously elaborated.* Literacy is absent from their H but literacy is not required to recognize a city as a city, a market as a market, or a hierarchy as a hierarchy. They would be illiterate but not existentially lost. The wonder comes from recognition cascading — every new thing resembles something they have a partial file for, scaled beyond what they imagined possible. That is a cognitively abundant experience, not a distressing one.

**Floridian in St. Louis: friction plus mild wistfulness.** Same language, overlapping culture, same political system, shared contemporary L-space. The H-topology mismatch is ecological, not categorical. The organizational logic of daily life parses correctly. The friction is at the level of environmental attunement — the body and the baseline were shaped by flat subtropical geography, springs, a tourism economy where most social transactions are with strangers who leave. St. Louis has an actual civic fabric, a permanent population, seasons, and no springs. The H parses the new L-space accurately but something in the environmental tuning is off. The homesickness is for a specific ecological register, not a different logical structure.

**The gradient:** confusion → grief → wonder → friction. Ordered by degree of H-topology overlap with the ambient L-space. At maximum mismatch (Jomon/Edo), the agent cannot parse events into coherent categories. At moderate mismatch (Native American/modern), the agent parses but the parsed world is wrong in a felt way. At partial overlap (Cahokia/modern), the agent parses with delight — recognition cascades through unfamiliar elaborations of familiar structures. At minimal mismatch (Floridian/St. Louis), the parsing is complete and the friction is somatic and ecological rather than cognitive.

**Plastic deformation.** With prolonged residence in a mismatched L-space, B(t) updates under λ, and new categorical content fills the Horizon. But the *topology* — the deep structure of what categories feel natural, what distinctions feel like real joints, what makes a scene parse immediately vs. require effort — updates more slowly and under a higher effective λ-threshold. This is why first-generation immigrants and second-generation immigrants have qualitatively different H-topologies even after decades. The content updates; the organizational geometry is slower. The deformation is plastic — permanent at sufficient magnitude and duration — but it is not instantaneous. You carry the original topology with you, expressed onto every new L-space you enter.

**"Sometimes crime."** When the carried H-topology is a poor fit to the dominant L-space's categorical structure, the natural joints in the agent's cognition do not align with the legal, social, and economic categories of the ambient system. Actions that are coherent within the carried topology — that feel like obvious moves in the space as the agent understands it — may cross categorical lines that do not exist in the carried H but are load-bearing in the ambient L-space. This is not a moral claim. It is a mechanistic description of what happens when two categorical geometries share the same physical territory. The friction becomes transgression when the misalignment lands on a category boundary the dominant L-space enforces.

The policy implication, which is not the framework's job to state but which follows: the relevant intervention is not moral instruction (which assumes the agent's H already contains the relevant categories as salient) but categorical bridge-building — creating shared structures that are reachable from both topologies.

## Relations to Existing Work

- **Zhang & Zahidi (2026)**: These four observations extend the taxonomy they are building. Point III gives the mechanism they lack for differential susceptibility to implantation. Point IV shows their implanted-memory category needs to be subdivided by Horizon precondition. They likely do not address cultural specificity.  
- **Jin (2026)**: Section V above gives the MBD interpretation of his deepest claim. The design perspective he applies to mental files lands in H-topology if you follow it through. His reference to natural kinds and property cluster structures is a claim about the local geometry of H, not just about content.
- **MBD Papers 1–6**: Points I and III are directly derivable from the core equation + κ dynamics. Points II, IV, and V are architectural constraints on H rather than derivations from the core equation.  
- **Fermi Solution (Paper 9)**: Point I (learned helplessness as prior-assigned near-zero probability) and Point III (κ-amplified confabulation as social vector for scapegoating) both have civilizational analogs — civilizations that have tried certain institutional experiments and failed may assign near-zero probability to re-attempting them, even when conditions change.
